Q18. Why JWT Instead Of Session-Based Authentication?

ROI: ★★★★★ Frequency: Extremely High

Short Answer

JWT supports stateless authentication which is more suitable for scalable distributed systems.

Detailed Answer

Traditional session-based authentication stores session data on the server. JWT stores authentication information inside a signed token. Advantages: 1. Stateless 2. Better scalability 3. Works well with microservices 4. Reduced server memory usage 5. Easier horizontal scaling

Interview Trap

Question: "If JWT is so great, why do many companies still use sessions?" Answer: Sessions provide easier invalidation and can be simpler for traditional web applications. JWT introduces challenges such as token revocation and refresh token management.

Q20. Why Role-Based Access Control?

ROI: ★★★★★ Frequency: High

Answer

Different user types require different permissions. For example: User: * Apply Job * Save Job Recruiter: * Create Job * Update Job * Delete Own Jobs Admin: * Full Platform Access RBAC centralizes authorization rules and simplifies maintenance.

Advanced Follow-Up

Would RBAC scale forever? No. Large systems often evolve toward: RBAC + Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC) for more fine-grained permissions.

Q23. Explain N+1 Query Problem

ROI: ★★★★★ Frequency: Extremely High

Answer

Suppose we load 100 jobs. For each job, Hibernate loads recruiter information separately. Instead of: 1 query We get: 1 + 100 queries This is called the N+1 Query Problem. Solutions: * Fetch Join * Entity Graph * DTO Projections * Batch Fetching

Interview Gold Point

Many performance issues in Spring applications originate from poorly managed entity relationships.

I understand budget considerations are important. My expectations are based on my experience, technical skills and the responsibilities associated with the role. That said, I remain open to discussing the overall package.

MANAGERIAL ROUND MASTERCLASS

Managerial rounds are less interested in syntax. Managers evaluate: * Ownership * Risk Management * Communication * Delivery * Leadership Potential

Q57. How Do You Estimate Tasks?

ROI: ★★★★★ Frequency: Extremely High

Answer

I break work into smaller components. For each component I consider: * Development * Testing * Code Review * Integration * Deployment * Risks I then include contingency for unknowns before providing estimates.
Q58. What Would You Do If A Critical Deadline Was At Risk?

ROI: ★★★★★ Frequency: Extremely High

Answer

First, I would identify the root cause. Next, I would assess impact and available options. I would communicate risks early, prioritize critical deliverables and collaborate with stakeholders to create a realistic plan. The goal is transparency and informed decision making.
Q59. How Do You Handle Technical Debt?

ROI: ★★★★★ Frequency: High

Answer

Technical debt should be managed proactively. I focus on: * Identifying debt early * Measuring impact * Prioritizing high-risk areas * Refactoring incrementally The goal is balancing business delivery with long-term maintainability.
Q60. How Do You Make Technical Decisions?

ROI: ★★★★★ Frequency: High

Answer

I evaluate: * Requirements * Trade-offs * Scalability * Maintainability * Cost * Risk The best technical decision is not always the most sophisticated one. It is the one that best satisfies business and engineering requirements.
Q61. What Would You Do If A Junior Developer Was Struggling?

ROI: ★★★★☆ Frequency: High

Answer

I would first understand the root cause. Possible causes include: * Lack of knowledge * Unclear requirements * Confidence issues * Environment challenges I would provide guidance, support and actionable feedback while encouraging independent problem-solving.
Q62. How Do You Balance Speed Versus Quality?

ROI: ★★★★★ Frequency: Extremely High

Answer

Both are important. The key is understanding business priorities and risks. Critical systems may require additional validation. Lower-risk features may prioritize faster delivery. Engineering decisions should align with business impact.

Q63. What Separates A Good Engineer From A Great Engineer?

ROI: ★★★★★ Frequency: Very High

Answer

A good engineer can solve assigned problems. A great engineer identifies problems before they become critical and proactively drives solutions. Good engineers focus on implementation. Great engineers understand business impact, scalability, reliability and long-term maintainability.
Q64. If You Joined Tomorrow, What Would You Focus On First?

ROI: ★★★★★ Frequency: High

Answer

My first priority would be understanding: * Business domain * Architecture * Team workflows * Deployment processes * Current challenges Before suggesting changes, I would spend time learning the existing system and identifying opportunities for improvement.
Q65. What Is The Biggest Technical Mistake You Frequently See?

ROI: ★★★★★ Frequency: High

Answer

One common mistake is optimizing for today's requirements without considering future maintainability. Examples: * Tight coupling * Poor separation of concerns * Lack of observability * Insufficient testing These decisions often create significant technical debt later.
Q66. How Would You Improve Engineering Productivity?

ROI: ★★★★★ Frequency: High

Answer

Engineering productivity improves when friction is reduced. Areas I would evaluate: * Build times * CI/CD efficiency * Testing automation * Documentation * Developer onboarding * Monitoring and observability Small improvements across these areas often produce significant gains.
Q67. What Would You Do If Production Failed At Midnight?

ROI: ★★★★★ Frequency: Extremely High

Answer

First priority is restoring service. My process: 1. Assess impact 2. Stabilize system 3. Communicate stakeholders 4. Investigate root cause 5. Implement permanent fix 6. Conduct postmortem During incidents, calm execution is more important than perfect execution.
Q68. How Do You Evaluate Trade-Offs?

ROI: ★★★★★ Frequency: Extremely High

Answer

Every engineering decision involves trade-offs. I typically evaluate: * Performance * Scalability * Maintainability * Complexity * Cost * Delivery Time The goal is finding the best balance for current business needs.
Q69. Have You Ever Challenged An Existing Design?

ROI: ★★★★☆ Frequency: High

Answer

Yes. When evaluating designs, I focus on evidence rather than opinion. If I identify concerns around scalability, reliability or maintainability, I present alternatives and discuss trade-offs with the team. The objective is improving outcomes, not winning arguments.
